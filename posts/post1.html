You know a steakhouse is serious the moment you step through the door. At Gallagher's, the first thing you see isn't a host stand or a coat check â€” it's the meat. A glass-fronted dry-aging room lines the entrance, and behind it hang enormous cuts of beef at various stages of their transformation: deep burgundy, marbled, dusted with the faint bloom of age. It's theatrical in the best way. This is their whole argument, right there in plain view before you've even been seated.

There's no pretense of a performance kitchen â€” these people are just cooking, and cooking well. The whole dining room smells faintly of woodsmoke and rendered fat, and it smells incredible.

Then the steaks arrived. We'd ordered a ribeye and a porterhouse to share, and both came out on the bone, perfectly charred on the outside and exactly medium-rare within. But the real story was the dry age.
